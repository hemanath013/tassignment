A food lover's guide to Ahmedabad

When it comes to gluttony, Ahmedabad is a true paradise

By Anil Mulchandani

20 July 2021

      From the majestic monuments of the walled city to the art galleries, trendy cafes and shopping streets, Ahmedabad has a gamut of offerings. But for those with large appetites, the city holds many dleights. If you're in the city for just two days, here's where you should go and what you should eat.

      48 hour guide to eating in Ahmedabad

      DAY 1: Morning

      The walled city of Ahmedabad—or the old city—is a hive of activity with colourful bazaars, workshops, mosques, temples and old residential quarters called pols. It is best to get here early before the streets get too crowded for a casual walk around.

      Start with breakfast at New Irani Restaurant near the Bhadra Fort. More than 60 years old and recently renovated, this café is extremely popular for its bun maska – you can see the workers slicing buns and buttering them near the arched entrance. If you are looking for a cooked breakfast, try the omelette and curries or a meat feast of paya, chicken masala and chaap.

      Digest breakfast by walking around Bhadra Fort, the citadel founded by Sultan Ahmed Shah in 1411 AD. There’s also Teen Darwaja, a triple archway built in the 15th century and Jama Masjid nearby, one of the most beautiful mosques in the country.

      Mid-morning: Feel like a cup of tea and a snack after the walk? Chandra Villas nearby is a restaurant that opened in 1898 and introduced the first ‘Gujarati thali’ of Ahmedabad in 1901. The restaurant has, unfortunately, lost its oak furniture and British fans to a fire, but it still remains as popular has been for over a century. Ask for the classic fafda-jalebi breakfast— the deep-fried crispy fafda pairs well with the sinfully sweet jalebi. It is served with a papaya relish called cheen, along with chopped onions, chillies and a kadhi-like dip. Down this with a masala chai before continuing to explore some of the `pols’ – these are narrow alleys lined with old houses. You can see exquisitely carved wooded facades of havelis, perhaps a colourful Jain or Hindu temple, an ornate bird-feeding structure called chabutra, and other remnants of life in the old city.


      From here, walk to the historical square called Manek Chowk. This forms the base to explore numerous markets: the Sindhi market for fabrics and sarees, Ratanpol where shop fronts showcase a variety from tailored shirts to colourful sarees, Zaveri Vad with its jewellery shops, Dalgharwad for textiles and garments, and others. But should you find your stomach rumbling again, the Bhatiyar Galli of Khas Bazaar near Manek Chowk is a meat lovers haven. Here you will find the cooks—known as Bhatiyaras frying meat on large tavas, ladling out meat on small plates, or making kebabs on charcoal stoves. Bera Samosa is one of the legendary names in this market—it is about a century old, and is famous for its spiced minced meat samosas that are often whisked away by food lovers to be eaten in the comfort of their homes. Vegetarians, check out Das Khaman, one of the famous places in the city for Gujarati farsan. About 100 years old, Das Khaman offers khaman, dhokla, khandvi and other local snacks.

      Afternoon 
      Head west across the Sabarmati to Tomato’s for lunch. Started in 1996, this restaurant has a 1950s-60s Americana theme with a superb collection of original posters of rock stars and Hollywood movies from the period, as well as props and American car models. Take a break from Gujarati cuisine and dig into the barbecue chicken wings, lemon-pepper chicken breast, vegetable skillet, and other classic American grub.

      Tomato’s is located on CG Road, which has many trendy stores, branded garment showrooms, boutiques and jewellery shops. Check out Jade Blue and Bandhej, which specialises in collections by NID-alumni Archana Shah made using handloom-woven fabrics embellished with crafts like embroidery, prints and spangles. Apart from these time-honoured classics, you can find some surprises like The Heritage Art Store that stocks cultural souvenirs that represent the rich heritage of Ahmedabad. The walls are lined with framed limited edition prints and embroidered wall panels, while tote bags and printed stoles hang on displays.

      Evening

      Head to Vishalla, a Gujarati-themed restaurant with traditionally decorated mud huts where artisans demonstrate their skills. It also has a performance area for music and dance. Don’t miss the Vechaar Utensils Museum, which features the owner Surendra Patel’s excellent collection of vessels used for cooking, religious rituals, storage, travel, milking cows and camels and a standout collection of ornate nutcrackers in various shapes and sizes.

      For dinner, settle down cross-legged on the floor in front of a platform where dhoti-clad waiters will quickly lay out a leaf platter, terracotta bowls and glasses that are deftly filled with authentic Gujarati rural food. If you are not in the mood for a full meal, they also have a snacks area. The Gujarati farsan and sweets like sukhdi and jalebi are superb, and you can wash it down with a seasonal drink or a cup of minty tea.

      DAY 2: Morning

      The University area of Ahmedabad has many impressive architectural buildings like the Indian Institute of Management (IIM-A) designed by Louis Kahn, the Achyut Kavinde-designed Ahmedabad Textile Industry's Research Association ( ATIRA) with a lush green campus, and the LD Institute of Indology designed by BV Doshi.

      From the University, it is a short drive to the sprawling BV Doshi-designed Shreyas Foundation’s Campus, which has lush gardens and bamboo groves. Fresh vegetables can be bought at the in-house farm here. You can also sign up for a course at the Vatsala Cooking School on campus.

      Ahmedabad locals love their ice cream, so if you are looking for a local favourite, head to Shankar’s Ice cream Library off Sindhu Bhawan Road (SBR). Started from a hand-cart, Shankar’s now has pleasant and well-lit interiors. You can choose from their wide range of flavours such as Alphonso Mango, Rose-and-Watermelon and Thandai.

      Afternoon

      Satiate the appetite you worked up with lunch at Gopi Dining Hall, a popular family dining spot at Prahladnagar. The thali here is a mix of flavours, colours and textures, with three or more vegetable dishes, a kathol (braised pulses like beans, chickpea or dry peas), a couple of yoghurt dishes like dahi, kadhi, raita or shrikhand, rice or khichdi, lentils (most often a toor dal made from pigeon peas), steamed and fried snacks, and sweets. Accompaniments include sweet, sour and spicy chutneys, pickles, ghee and a salad of chopped vegetables served raw or may be steamed in spices. Beverages, like chaas and aam ras in summer, are also served. The meals are limitless with waiters coming up with generous refills at regular intervals.

      Take the road to Sabarmati Ashram where Mahatma Gandhi lived and worked from 1917 to 1930. Here, you can see his former residence, Hriday Kunj that houses his simple belongings. The museum designed by Charles Correa offers an interesting insight into the life and philosophies of Mahatma Gandhi. You can also check out Sanskar Kendra, the museum building designed by Le Corbusier which offers an insight into the history and cultural diversity of Ahmedabad, and a collection of kites.

      Evening

      For your afternoon tea or coffee, visit the Welcomheritage Mani Mansion. This heritage house built in 1923 houses a charming garden café called Fresh Roast. Refresh yourself with an iced coffee and a couple of snacks here. If the owner is around, you can request a tour of the hotel that will be peppered with interesting anecdotes.

      Then, head to the Sabarmati Riverfront to catch the sunset. This remarkable development of urban planning has converted the two banks of the river in the city of Ahmedabad into a multi-purpose space with parks, gardens, buildings, food zones, recreational facilities, event venues and exhibition spaces.

      For dinner, head to Agashiye, the rooftop restaurant of the House of MG, a heritage hotel comprising a pair of early-20th century Indo-European style mansions. Agashiye is known for its elaborate thali dinner that features vegetarian regional dishes of Gujarat. If you are not in the mood for an entire thali, The Green House is their coffee shop where you can munch on Gujarati snacks, sweets and hand-churned ice cream.
